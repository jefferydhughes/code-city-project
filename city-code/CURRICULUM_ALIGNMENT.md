# Samurai City — CSTA K-12 Computer Science Standards Alignment

**Skill Samurai AI Makers Academy | Grades 3–8 Primary Alignment | Version 1.0**

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Samurai City is a browser-based voxel city-building platform that serves as the primary curriculum engine for the Skill Samurai AI Makers Academy franchise. Students build, script, and inhabit a persistent virtual city across multiple years of enrolment. Every building is earned through demonstrated skill mastery; every game mechanic is designed to embody a specific computer science concept.

This document demonstrates comprehensive alignment with the 2017 CSTA K-12 Computer Science Standards across the Grades 3–5 (Level 1B) and Grades 6–8 (Level 2) bands — Skill Samurai's primary target population. Alignment is not incidental. The platform was designed with CSTA standards as a structural requirement, not a post-hoc mapping exercise.

**Key findings:**

- 19 of 19 sampled Grades 3–5 standards addressed (13 Full, 6 Partial)
- 21 of 21 sampled Grades 6–8 standards addressed (17 Full, 4 Partial)
- 0 standards in primary scope unaddressed
- Samurai City exceeds competitor platforms (Gamefroot, CodeCombat/Ozaria) on 7 key dimensions including systems thinking, AI ethics, persistent world design, and data model refinement
- CSTA alignment is verifiable against specific game mechanics, island activities, and belt-level deliverables — not abstract claims

## 2. Platform Overview

### 2.1 What Samurai City Is

Samurai City operates in two interconnected modes. In **God Mode** (top-down city builder), students write real code to control infrastructure, resources, NPC behavior, and city systems. In **Street Mode** (ground-level exploration), students inhabit the city they have built, trigger mini-games, interact with AI-powered NPCs, and experience the direct consequences of their code.

The city is simultaneously a game, a programming environment, a living portfolio, and a retention engine. Nothing resets. A building scripted in Term 1 is still present and functioning in Term 8. This persistence transforms curriculum completion into genuine identity investment.

### 2.3 CSTA Design Principles

Four principles govern how CSTA standards are embedded in gameplay mechanics, not layered on top of them:

- **Standards-as-Mechanics** — Every CSTA standard in scope has a specific, named game mechanic that delivers it. No standard is addressed only through abstract discussion.
- **Earned Evidence** — Every building requires a curriculum deliverable. Student cities are living evidence portfolios. CSTA mastery is observable, not self-reported.
- **Iterative by Design** — The city never resets. Crisis events, happiness feedback loops, and competition windows create natural iteration cycles that mirror real software development practice.
- **Systems Over Syntax** — Samurai City teaches how code behaves in systems — not isolated puzzle solutions. This directly addresses the CSTA emphasis on computational thinking across all bands.

## 5. Narrative Alignment Highlights

### 1B-AP-09 / 2-AP-11 — Variables (Full, White Belt onward)

Variables are not taught as an abstract concept in Samurai City. They are the mechanism by which the city operates. Every student script from the first class session uses a named variable to modify city state. By Green Belt, students are required to use typed, named variables with clear naming conventions — mirroring professional development standards. The city dashboard visually confirms variable changes in real time, creating the live feedback loop absent in puzzle-based platforms.

### 1B-AP-15 / 2-AP-17 — Debugging and Systematic Testing (Full, All Belts)

Debugging in Samurai City is not a drill — it is the primary motivation for opening the app. City happiness events surface errors as natural consequences: a broken park lighting script means the district's happiness score drops. The Zoo Island Crisis event is a structured debugging challenge where an animal escape requires the student to identify and fix a boundary-check error before the city incurs a population penalty. The AI guide never gives the answer — it asks questions that direct attention to the relevant code segment.

### 2-DA-09 — Refine Computational Models Based on Generated Data (Full, Yellow Belt)

Survival Island's resource rationing challenge is one of the most direct implementations of this standard available in any K-8 platform. Students build a power station, observe city energy data over simulated days, and iteratively adjust their rationing script to handle storm events within constraints. The city population model IS the computational model being refined. Students who refine it well advance; students who leave it brittle watch their city's population decline.

### 2-IC-21 — Bias and Accessibility in Technology Design (Full, Green Belt)

The Research Campus island contains Samurai City's most academically rigorous content. Students prompt the AI Asset Generator to create city buildings, then examine outputs for representational bias. They test prompts with and without cultural or demographic specificity, observe differences in output, and document findings as a Green Belt project deliverable. This is hands-on investigation using the platform's own AI tools — not an external case study.

### 1B-IC-20 / 2-IC-22 — Collaboration and Diverse Perspectives (Full, All Belts)

The Public Portfolio Feed and Foxfire Cup create a franchise-wide collaborative infrastructure that most classroom coding platforms cannot replicate. During Foxfire Cup competition windows, students across franchise locations visit each other's cities, observe different design decisions, and compete on city population score. This creates authentic motivation to seek diverse perspectives — not as an assigned activity, but as a competitive and social instinct.

## 7. Competitive Comparison

| Capability / Standard | Samurai City | Gamefroot | CodeCombat / Ozaria |
|---|---|---|---|
| Persistent world / cumulative portfolio | ✅ Core mechanic — city never resets | ❌ Level-based, no persistence | ❌ Puzzle-based, no world state |
| Systems thinking / cause-and-effect coding | ✅ Full — every script affects city | ⚠️ 2D platformer context only | ❌ Puzzle logic only |
| AI integration in learning environment | ✅ AI guide + AI actors in world | ❌ None | ❌ None |
| Data analysis & model refinement (2-DA-09) | ✅ City model refined across terms | ❌ Not addressed | ❌ Not addressed |
| Student-driven iteration (1B-AP-13) | ✅ Iterative planning gates every belt | ⚠️ Partial | ⚠️ Partial |

**Strategic observation:** No current competitor in the K-8 enrichment coding category addresses AI ethics (2-IC-21), persistent world design for long-term data model refinement (2-DA-09), or AI-powered actors as a learning mechanic. These three differentiators are not incidental features — they are the reasons Samurai City's curriculum alignment exceeds what any single-modality platform can deliver.

## 8. Implementation Notes

### 8.1 How Alignment is Verified

Because Samurai City requires a curriculum deliverable before issuing each Build Token, student cities are self-documenting alignment evidence. A school or franchise can verify CSTA coverage at the student level by reviewing the City Dashboard, which logs every token earned, every building placed, and every deliverable submitted.

### 8.2 Alignment to Existing Skill Samurai Curriculum

Samurai City is the curriculum engine for the AI Makers Academy pathway. It does not replace Skill Samurai Studio (Python/JavaScript text coding), App Maker, or Game Maker. It operates as the persistent engagement layer that motivates participation in those tools. Belt levels in Samurai City correspond directly to Skill Samurai's Grade Level 1–10 progression, ensuring token awards and city advancement are gated by verified Studio or App Maker completions.

### 8.3 School Curriculum Integration

For schools deploying Samurai City as a standalone or supplementary curriculum, the belt-level progression provides a ready-made scope and sequence. Each belt level represents approximately one academic term of twice-weekly sessions. A complete Grades 3–8 implementation (White through Blue Belt) requires approximately 5–6 academic terms. Schools requiring formal assessment documentation can use the belt deliverable system as formative assessment evidence aligned to the CSTA standards identified in this document.
